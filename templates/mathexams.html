State Exam Scores

Students in New York City (along with the rest of the state) take state exams every year from 3rd grade to 8th grade, one for English, and one for Math. We analyzed data of the percentage of students scoring in each threshold in the math state exam based on three factors; race, gender, and economic standing. Some pretty interesting patterns were found.

Economic Standpoint

From the graphs below, we can see a very clear positive correlation of a student's economic standpoint and their test scores. The scores are split off into 4 levels, with 1 being the lowest, and 4 being the highest. Those who are economically disadvantaged have lower average test scores than those who are well off. For 4 of the boroughs, this attribute holds true, but for The Bronx, we have a special case. Even students who are economically stable still have a higher chance on average of scoring within the range of a level 1. This could be due to other factors like the poverty level of the Bronx, or the amount of Black and Hispanic people living in the Bronx (numbers have shown that they tend to score lower on tests). In the span of 5 years, the gap has closed at varying rates depending on the borough. This is most likely due to changes in curriculum, changes in the test, or just plain improvement in the performance of students.

Gender

According to the score chart based on gender, boys tend to score higher on average than girls, and that trend stays constant for all 5 years. However, What's surprising is how quickly the ratios of the score ranges for girls evened out. For example, in 2015, about 77% of girls in Manhattan scored within the range of a level 2 or 1, but by 2019, that number had decreased to about 57%, with about 43% scoring within a 3 or 4. This trend is present in all 5 boroughs, which also could be an effect of a change in the curriculum, a change in the test, or an improvement in the performance of girls in school.

Racial Group

The biggest disproportionality is in the racial data. White and Asian students have been performing considerably better than Hispanic and Black students for the past 5 years. and in 2019, a larger portion of Black and Hispanic students earned scores in the level 1 range than higher score ranges, which is a troubling parameter. These low scores could be a product of a lack of guidance and motivation. Blacks and Hispanics are extremely underrepresented in the STEM field, and kids may not have a role model or guide to push them to do well on things like exams that you may need to excel in to have a career in STEM.
